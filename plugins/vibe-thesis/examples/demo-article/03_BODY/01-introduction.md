# Introduction

The practice is well-known [claim:1]. A programmer, stuck on a bug, places
a small yellow rubber duck on the desk. They explain the code to the duck —
line by line, sometimes function by function. Often, midway through the
explanation, they stop, look at the duck, and discover the bug. The duck
has not moved. It has not blinked. It has not asked a single question.

The earliest popularization of the practice in the developer literature
appears in [@hunt1999pragmatic], where it is offered as a small protocol
under the heading "Rubber Ducking." The lore is older — programmers have
been talking to inert objects since at least the early days of timesharing,
when the only available listener might be a teletype, a cup of coffee, or
the wall.

What is happening here? The duck does not provide feedback. It does not
encode the programmer's domain knowledge. It is, at most, a focal point —
something to address.

This article takes the practice seriously enough to ask what the duck is
actually for. The argument, briefly: the duck is not the mechanism
[claim:2]. The mechanism is articulation — the act of putting implicit
understanding into explicit language. The duck is a cheap audience that
licenses articulation in private. Anything that licenses articulation
will work; the duck is convenient because it is silent.

What follows is not an empirical study. It is a short synthesis: practice
description, two pieces of theoretical framing borrowed from work on
programming as theory-building [@naur1985programming] and cognitive
offloading [@norman1988design], and a small taxonomy of what the duck
does for the programmer in the moment of explanation.
