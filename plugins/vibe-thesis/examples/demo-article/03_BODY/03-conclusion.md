# A Small Taxonomy

Three frames seem to capture what the duck does for the programmer in the
moment of explanation.

**The duck as cognitive scaffold.** [@norman1988design] argues that designed
objects redistribute cognitive load — a door's handle tells you to pull, a
sign tells you which way to walk. The duck is an extreme case: an object
whose entire affordance is "address me." It offloads the question of *who*
to articulate to, leaving the programmer free to focus on *what* to
articulate. The handle of cognition is the duck's silent attention
[claim:3].

**The duck as audience-of-one.** Articulation has a social dimension —
talking aloud to no one feels strange in a way that talking aloud to a duck
does not. The duck satisfies the social precondition of articulation
without imposing the social cost (the colleague's time, the meeting room's
schedule, the embarrassment of asking). It is the cheapest possible
audience.

**The duck as commitment device.** Placing the duck on the desk is a small
ritual that says *now I am going to debug by talking it through*. The duck
makes the protocol visible to the programmer themselves, which makes it
easier to commit to. Pair programming and code review serve the same
commitment-device role at higher cost [claim:4]; the duck is the lowest
cost, lowest commitment version of the same move.

What makes a duck worth talking to is not the duck. It is the requirement
to articulate — and the recognition that the programmer's own cognition
benefits from being externalized into language a listener could follow,
even if the listener is a piece of yellow plastic.

Teaching someone to debug, then, is teaching the articulation. With or
without a duck. The discipline of literate programming [@knuth1984literate]
is the same move written down — the documentation IS the articulation,
made permanent and inspectable [claim:5]. The rubber duck and the literate
program are the same practice, separated by whether the articulation is
ephemeral or archived.

Future work might examine whether AI coding assistants displace or augment
the duck. The early evidence suggests augmentation: the assistant is a
duck that occasionally talks back, and the conversation form preserves the
articulation work even when the assistant's responses are wrong.
