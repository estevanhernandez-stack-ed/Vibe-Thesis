# Proposal — A Brief Examination of Rubber Ducks in Software Engineering

## The question

When a programmer explains a problem to an inert object — most famously a
yellow rubber duck — and the explanation itself surfaces the bug, what is
actually happening? Is the duck a passive prop, an externalization device,
or something stranger?

This article surveys the practice (sometimes called *rubber duck
debugging*), traces its lineage from informal lore to its appearance in
[@hunt1999pragmatic], and proposes a small taxonomy of what the duck *does*
for the programmer in the moment of explanation.

## Scope

A short-form article, suitable for a developer-facing publication or a
conference lightning-talk write-up. No new empirical study; this is
synthesis of existing practice descriptions plus a light theoretical frame
borrowed from [@naur1985programming] on programming as theory-building and
[@norman1988design] on cognitive offloading via designed objects.

## Why this is low-stakes

The article is intentionally uncontroversial. Rubber duck debugging is a
real practice, lightly documented, well-known among programmers. The point
of the demo is to round-trip through the render pipeline and produce a
publishable-looking PDF — not to advance a frontier claim.
