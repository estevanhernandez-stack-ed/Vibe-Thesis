# Results and Discussion

We present three case studies, each illustrating one infrastructure dimension and its argumentative consequences.

## Case 1 — Citation Regime

The choice of citation regime — author-date versus footnote — affects which kinds of evidence appear citable. In the works examined, footnote regimes correlate with denser engagement with secondary literature; author-date regimes correlate with cleaner argumentative spines but thinner contextual engagement [@goossens1994].

This is not a verdict on which regime is "better" — both serve their fields. The point is that the regime is not neutral. Authors switching fields often inherit a regime that doesn't serve their argumentative goals; recognizing the choice as a choice opens up moves they otherwise wouldn't consider.

## Case 2 — Sectional Structure

Conventional sectional structure (Introduction / Method / Results / Discussion) carries an implicit argumentative shape. Departures from convention require justification but unlock moves the convention forecloses [@wadler1989].

We examined six works that deviate from conventional structure (extended methodological sections, reverse-order presentation, integrated results+discussion). In each case, the structural deviation supported an argumentative claim that conventional structure would have buried. The cost was reader friction; the benefit was argumentative clarity for the specific claim.

## Case 3 — Visual Apparatus

Figures and tables are typically treated as evidence presentations. Our cases suggest they can also do argumentative work — establishing a frame, defining terms, structuring comparison. This is more visible in scientific writing but is present in humanities work as well, often unrecognized.

## Discussion

These cases support a modest claim: writing infrastructure decisions are argumentative decisions, even when they appear technical. They constrain what feels natural to write; they enable some moves and foreclose others.

The claim is local to the cases examined. Generalizing across fields, time periods, and document types requires further work. But within the cases — humanities scholarship from the late 20th and early 21st centuries — the pattern is clear enough to warrant the attention of authors making infrastructure decisions for their own work.
