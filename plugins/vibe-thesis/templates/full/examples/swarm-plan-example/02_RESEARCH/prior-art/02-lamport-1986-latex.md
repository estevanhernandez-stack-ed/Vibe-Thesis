# Lamport, *LaTeX: A Document Preparation System* (1986)

**Citation:** Lamport, Leslie. *LaTeX: A Document Preparation System.* Reading, MA: Addison-Wesley, 1986.

## One-paragraph summary

Lamport's manual for LaTeX, the macro-package built on top of Knuth's TeX. Where Knuth's system exposes maximum typographic control to the author, Lamport's LaTeX abstracts much of it away in favor of structural markup — the author specifies *what* (chapter, section, citation) and the system decides *how* it renders. The shift from TeX to LaTeX is itself an interesting case in our terms: the same underlying capability, two different stances on whether infrastructure decisions belong to the author.

## Main claim

Authors should focus on document structure (logical organization) and let the typesetting system handle visual rendering. Separation of concerns between author and typesetter is productive.

## Connection to thesis

Complicates the thesis. Lamport's stance is the *opposite* of Knuth's — and arguably the dominant stance in current academic writing. If LaTeX's "structure not visuals" approach is right, infrastructure isn't argumentative; it's neutral plumbing the author shouldn't worry about.

We need to engage this position directly in the lit review and address why the stance breaks down for serious arguments. (Likely answer: the abstraction is *almost* sufficient, but the cases where it's insufficient are exactly the cases where infrastructure carries argumentative load.)
