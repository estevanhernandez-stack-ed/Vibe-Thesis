# Results

## Theoretical Implications

If authors retain agency over infrastructure decisions — as the empirical findings suggest — this complicates the standard account of academic writing as field-conformity-driven. The standard account treats infrastructure as inherited; our findings suggest it's actively chosen.

This matters for three reasons. First, it relocates responsibility: authors are not victims of their fields' conventions, they are participants in shaping them. Second, it opens a normative question: what choices serve which argumentative goals? Third, it suggests the conventions themselves are downstream of accumulated individual choices, not upstream.

## Limits of the Inference

The inference from "within-field variance exists" to "authors retain agency" is not airtight. Within-field variance could reflect institutional pressure (different departments enforcing different norms), publisher requirements (different journals within a field having different house styles), or generational drift rather than individual authorial choice.

Our data cannot fully distinguish these causes. We treat the agency interpretation as the most parsimonious; readers may reasonably weight the alternatives differently.
