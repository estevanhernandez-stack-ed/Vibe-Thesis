# Methodology {#sec:methodology}

The framework introduced in [@sec:introduction] requires a method for identifying infrastructure decisions in published academic work. This chapter develops that method in three stages: source selection, decision identification, and effect attribution.

## Source Selection

We draw our corpus from journals and monographs published between 1980 and 2024 in three subfields. Selection criteria:

1. **Reputation within field** — publications recognized as canonical or significant by at least three independent secondary sources.
2. **Methodological diversity** — the corpus deliberately spans different methodological traditions (qualitative, quantitative, hermeneutic) to avoid over-fitting to one style.
3. **Authorial reflection** — preference for works whose authors have published reflections (interviews, essays, prefaces) about their composition process; these reflections ground the otherwise inferential analysis.

The final corpus contains 47 works distributed unevenly across the three subfields: 18 from subfield A, 16 from subfield B, 13 from subfield C.

## Decision Identification

For each work in the corpus, we identify infrastructure decisions across four dimensions:

- **Citation regime** — the style and density of citations; how the work treats secondary literature
- **Sectional structure** — chapter organization, section transitions, presence of methodological apparatus
- **Visual apparatus** — figures, tables, footnote/endnote choice, use of formal notation
- **Genre conformity** — degree to which the work adheres to or departs from its field's conventional structure

Decisions are coded by two readers independently; disagreements are resolved through discussion or, where unresolvable, flagged in the data as contested. Following [@knuth1984], we treat the appearance of typographic deliberation — consistent unconventional choices that suggest authorial intent — as evidence of decision rather than accident.

## Effect Attribution

Linking infrastructure decisions to argumentative effects is the methodologically delicate step. We adopt three strategies, none of which is sufficient alone:

1. **Authorial testimony** — where an author has explicitly linked an infrastructure choice to an argumentative goal, we accept the link with appropriate skepticism.
2. **Contrastive analysis** — comparing works that make different choices to argue similar positions; differences in argumentative texture become attributable to the differences in infrastructure.
3. **Interpretive reconstruction** — where neither testimony nor contrast is available, we propose readings that connect choice to effect, marking these as inference rather than observation.

The interpretive reconstruction strategy is the weakest and is used sparingly. Where we deploy it, we present the alternative readings that survive the same evidence — letting the reader judge whether our preferred interpretation is the most defensible.

## Limitations

Three limitations bear mention. First, the corpus is small relative to the universe of published academic writing; generalization beyond the corpus is hazardous. Second, the four-dimensional coding scheme imposes a structure that may not capture all consequential infrastructure decisions; we likely under-detect choices we lack categories for. Third, our interpretive reconstructions necessarily reflect our own reading practices; another team applying the same method might surface different decisions and different effects.

We address these limitations by treating our findings as illustrative rather than definitive — claims about *what is possible* in the relationship between infrastructure and argument, not claims about *what is universal*.
