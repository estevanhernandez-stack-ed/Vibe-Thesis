# Introduction {#sec:introduction}

The structured composition of long-form academic writing has occupied scholars across multiple traditions [@knuth1984; @lamport1986]. This dissertation engages with that lineage by examining the relationship between document infrastructure and argumentative coherence — a relationship that the typesetting community has theorized but the writing-process community has only intermittently addressed.

## The Problem

Existing accounts of academic composition treat formatting and argumentation as separable concerns. Style guides specify how to render a citation; writing handbooks specify how to construct an argument. The gap between them is rarely mapped, and when it is, the mapping is informal at best [@goossens1994].

This work argues that the separation is artificial. The choice of citation style shapes which kinds of evidence appear citable; the choice of document structure shapes which kinds of argument feel natural to construct. We examine three cases where infrastructure decisions have demonstrably constrained or enabled argumentative moves.

## Scope and Limits

We restrict our analysis to scholarly writing in the humanities and humanistic social sciences from 1980 to the present. Earlier periods, and the natural sciences more broadly, exhibit different patterns we do not address here. The dataset is therefore neither comprehensive nor universally generalizable; the claims are local to the period and traditions we examine.

We also acknowledge a methodological limit: our analysis depends on retrospective reconstruction of decisions made by past authors. Where we cannot recover an author's stated rationale, we infer from textual artifacts — an interpretive move that introduces uncertainty we flag in the relevant chapters [@wadler1989].

## Structure of the Dissertation

Chapter 2 ([@sec:methodology]) develops the methodological framework: how we identify infrastructure decisions in published work, and how we link those decisions to argumentative effects. Subsequent chapters apply the framework to three case studies, with a concluding chapter assessing what generalizable claims survive contact with the cases.

The contribution is modest: we do not propose a new theory of academic writing. We propose a way of *seeing* the writing infrastructure as itself argumentative — and we work through what falls out of that lens.
